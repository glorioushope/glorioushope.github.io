GH 2016 Mid-Run Fellowship

Words by Diwata, a GH Life Coach

Pizza. Cake. Litson. It was as if there was a feast at Glorious Hope last Saturday, August 13, 2016. Hawaiian. Cowboys. Princesses. These were some of the many themes that the participants thought of as they interpreted this year’s theme for the GH 2016 Mid-Run Fellowship, which is “Gear Up for a Breakthrough: Be Transformed from the inside Out”.


“How has Glorious Hope impacted my life?”

Accepted. This was how I felt when I joined Glorious Hope even when I was rejected by my own friends and family. Nonjudgmental. This was how my life coaches and my co-participants were as I shared with them the poor decisions I made and the stories that I have kept buried in a dark place so that no one would see them. Significant. To the Glorious Hope family, I was, even when my own parents ignored me and told me that I was hopeless.


More than all these, I felt loved. I was reminded that the Lord loves me and still, He pursues me in spite of my inadequacy and all my failures. He used my life coaches to make me feel His love as they patiently and lovingly listened, asked follow-up questions, and helped me in my healing journey.


“I want in!”

Due to the comfort that God gave me through Glorious Hope, I was inspired to share the same comfort that I experienced in the Glorious Hope family. When the paper was being passed on as Glorious Hope asked for volunteers, I didn’t hesitate to sign up. I know that there are a lot of hurting people out there. I hope that just like my life coach, I would also be used by God to share the same comfort that He has given me.


I am joyful that in spite of my imperfections, the Lord has allowed me to serve Him as a life coach in Glorious Hope. He has given me the privilege to be a part of other people’s lives as they share their stories with me – their hurts, their hearts. More importantly, God has given me the opportunity to share the love and hope that could only come from Him and Him alone.


To give you a glimpse of our Glorious Hope family, watch the short video of the GH 2016 Mid-Run Fellowship below.

“So, what’s next for you?”

A whole lot. That is the number of people out there who are hurting both publicly and privately. Many feel lost, confused, imprisoned by their own bitterness, anger, and resentment. Many do not know how to cope. Many do not know the truth that there is hope, that change is possible.


Now, you know that change is possible. Now, you know that there is hope in Jesus. Now, you know that you can make a difference. You might think, “But I still have so many things going on in my life”. God is in control. Still is. Always is. If you are willing to be used as a channel of God’s love, believe me when I say that He is faithful until the end.

Digging for Desire – Part 1

Words by Coco, a GH Participant

I’ll tell you what I want, what I really really want, 

So tell me what you want, what you really really want, 

I wanna, I wanna, I wanna, I wanna, I wanna really really really wanna zigazig ha. 

-Spice Girls


What do you want? What do you really, really want?


I got employed in a casino when I was 23 and for several years, I did nothing but shuffle cards, deal and pay. Four or five years into the job, I was handed opportunities to do some hosting and write for the newsletter. It was a welcome respite from the hustle and bustle of facilitating card games, but something in me was amiss. My days and nights as a casino dealer drifted away on autopilot until I got suspended.


In the interim, I applied for a part-time hosting job in an events company. I lacked proper training, was too old and inexperienced but for some reason, I got hired. That day, when the CEO rhetorically asked me and the rest of the hopeful hosts/actors, “What do you want? What do you really, really want?” I knew I wanted the job. I wanted it because it awakened something in me. I wanted it for the joy of bringing a character to life – from a white-faced clown with a plump red nose to a pretty fairy with lovely locks and everything else in between. It mattered to me that I was able to express myself while ushering spectators onto a happy reality. It mattered to me that I was valued for my character, not for what I did.


When my days of suspension were over, I reported back to the exciting but mechanical reality of the casino grind.

I was 11, so young, naïve and idealistic, when I first fell in love with teatro. Countless rehearsals birthed in me the dream to pursue a course, if any, on theatre. I wanted the fairy’s role but I wasn’t that pretty and I didn’t know ballet so the director cast me as another character. I must have been very good at it because I was awarded a gold medal for my performance as the animated garbage pile.

My parents didn’t enroll me in theatre school, but I passed when I auditioned for membership in a university-wide theatre guild. Allow me to say that life, unfortunately, propelled me onto something I didn’t want or aspired for. I pursued a major that I tried hard to be good at because my parents deemed it more job-worthy than pursuing the arts. So today, almost three decades later, I sometimes tend to be unsure of my talents, and of myself.

Wait. Have I not made a decision to stop blaming my parents for my unrealized dreams and unmet desires and start taking responsibility for my actions? Have I not already chosen to relish life as it is with all its bitter and sweet memories (and would-be lessons) together? Have I not turned my life over – my past, present and future – to the LORD Jesus Christ?


Yes, I have. And I want – I really, really want – to keep on moving forward, live a life of purpose and be the person God wants me to be.

Relish

Words by Coco, a GH Participant

It didn’t stop there.


You probably think that by now I would’ve already gotten over my past (for what it’s worth) and moved on. For a great deal of my hurts, yes, I already have.


I am free to be me, yes, but becoming the person I am meant to be? That’s not easy – I don’t even know exactly how I’d look like when I do. Old with gray hair and wrinkles, perhaps, but who can tell?


So to keep me going, I’ve come to think of recovery as eating a slice of rainbow cake with its layers of tastes and colors. I mean, how do you usually eat a cake? You take bite sizes, right? You can’t eat the whole thing – not even a slice – all at once.


I am learning, that my trust issues and my fear of abandonment are rooted in some of my painful experiences growing up. I don’t remember my parents making any promises they didn’t keep; but I can vividly recall the things they’ve done which left an indelible mark of doubt in my young heart. Like when Papa would be away for weeks or even months and I didn’t know when he’ll come back. Or when he didn’t show up for my summer graduation. Those things stayed with me. Well, some happy events in our family stayed with me, too, and those are the ones I hope to remember long enough to tell my own grandchildren.

In Glorious Hope, as I take each bite of my flavored past, I learn to relish the bitter memories and its sweet lessons together, layer by layer.

As I answered the questions in Lessons 3 and 4, I realized that the doubt I feel today, in essence, is disobedience and it hinders me from being fully restored by God into sanity.

When I doubt, I am choosing not to trust God, anyone – not even myself – but I am unwittingly making myself a god (small “g”) doing as I want, not as God would want. This brings to me to an unhealthy state of being angry, lonely and negativistic instead of happy, hopeful and happy. Oh, did I say “happy” twice?

I’m not much of a Harry Potter fan but I couldn’t agree more with what J.K. Rowling shared to the graduates of Harvard University in 2008:

“There is an expiry date on blaming your parents for steering you in the wrong direction; the moment you are old enough to take the wheel, responsibility lies with you.”

Tears bring healing, I am told, but to soak up in them over and over and over again isn’t. I have to stop, eventually. Resentment is a bitter juice. I need to make a choice against drowning myself in tears, wallowing in regret over the good words I should have told my parents, the moments I would have enjoyed with our family, or the choices I could have made instead. 


My father is long gone, the past is done. I cannot change my parents nor my heritage. But I can do something today for my tomorrows. My mom is still here. I have a loving husband. It is high time that I take responsibility for my actions and own up to every decision I’ve made or will make.


When doubt freezes me on my tracks, I can choose to thaw my thoughts through prayer.


When fear blocks my view of the rainbow in the clouds, I can choose to readjust my lenses and focus on the goodness of God.


When condemnation seeps through my blanket of security, I can choose to let myself be embraced in the love and forgiveness God grants me through Christ alone.


Now. Since I can’t have my cake and eat it at the same time, I can choose to simply relish every bite of it, can’t I?

Reli(e)ving the Pain

Words by Coco, a GH Participant

He was a tower over her, but frail, still recovering from weeks of hospital confinement. Her vestida, meanwhile, made her look wider than she actually was.


She pounced on and hit him in angst-ridden pain. He didn’t move. He didn’t fight back. He didn’t say a word but only tried to contain her on the shoulders.


I quietly watched her hysterics with my teenage eyes. On his face, I only saw pain.


“Why is she doing that? Doesn’t she care that he’s sick?” I thought. I felt sorry for him. I couldn’t help him. I couldn’t help them.


I didn’t want to go back to that hateful memory. I loved him. I loved her – and I love her even more today. Still, nostalgia got the better of me. I was my teenage self, sitting on my Papa’s chair, watching my Mama beat him like crazy. The feeling came back like an old friend: resentment.


I remember some of his stories and how he sang until I fell asleep. I remember how he very quietly slipped a gift into the tiny Christmas socks on my bedside. I remember how, when I was 12, we scoured the metro just to find a copy of my Science book and he got late for work because of that.


For me, he was the finest gentleman any girl could ever have for a father. He valued education. He didn’t throw his weight around but carried himself well. He regarded all work with diligence and dignity – from carpentry to finishing an article to creating a blueprint. Always neatly dressed, he spoke with candor and eloquence that some of his students and colleagues hated him for it.


I always looked forward to when he would come home from work so I can give him his slippers and he can hand me his pasalubong – even if it was just a few chocolate trinkets. 

It made me happy seeing his handwriting on the postcards he sent from places he had been to. He provided for our needs the best he could. He taught us how to pray and even when he didn’t teach us how to read the Bible, I saw him read it almost daily.

He’s long been gone but my love for him never waned. His voice, his embrace, the first time he let me sit on his lap when I was 5 and how, a few days before he died he told me over the phone, “Love na love ka ni Papa.” Papa loves you very much.

I didn’t realize I still resented my mother. I remember saying to my young self that time, “I don’t want to be like her.” She’s a very good mother but not a very respectful wife, I thought. I also wasn’t aware that my heart still grieved for my father. Even when I loved both my parents, in my heart I didn’t want my love story – my marriage – to be like theirs.

The dam broke, the tears flowed. As my life coach walked me through our homework in Lesson 2, I knew I have stepped on the road towards my complete healing from the dysfunctions of our family. The rug which covered my pain, my resentment, has been removed. I had to relive the pain so that I can learn from it. I had to relive the pain so I can be fully, eventually, relieved of it.

Free to be Me

Words by Coco, a GH Life Participant

The notification popped up almost at the same time as I finished writing “Mirrors.” Yup, seriously. It was from Plymouth (pronounced: plee-muth), my ex-boyfriend.


Several years ago, before – and even during – Glorious Hope sessions, I would feel angry-sad whenever Ply’s memory, look-alike, namesake (or anything which sounds like Ply’s name) would come up. Last week, however, there was no such emotion. I didn’t feel angry nor sad. I didn’t feel excited either. At best, I’d say I felt amused. It has been 12 years since we broke up so it’s interesting that Ply still remembers me.


When my life coach asked me to share my answers in the Lesson 1 homework, I realized that there was no more pain in my heart towards Ply. Wow! After four Glorious Hope runs, I can now honestly say that there is no more tinge of guilt in my heart over the sins my ex and I committed together against God and against each other. No more anger towards myself nor Ply. I’ve forgiven Plymouth. I’ve forgiven myself. I’ve moved on. I am healed, I am free!


Still, I can’t say I’ve already arrived. My journey is far from over. I’m still near the shore. I need to dive, swim deeper and get to the bottom of my dysfunctions – my quirks, attitude problems and crazy-angry episodes. More importantly, I am yet to uncover – or discover – where the murmuring waves of doubt in my heart come from.


My orange workbook beckons. Same words, different lesson: WRITE ABOUT IT.


Writing helps me organize my thoughts and be more objective about my recovery. I can be talkative so writing cuts down my talk time – I only need to read and not blabber about my answers. It also allows more air time for the other participants in our group to share.


I flip a few pages back:

All dysfunctions are birthed in the family.

At this point, I wonder which of my quirks I could have inherited from my parents or grandparents? Ahh… Having homeworks is like going back to school again – only this time, the subject is myself.


I like the culture of honesty in the Glorious Hope program. I am learning how best to listen and not pass judgment on someone else. I can share my pains and struggles without being bashed. I can open up without being rejected. I can speak freely and be genuinely listened to. I am free to be me.

Mirrors

Words by Coco, a GH Participant

“How many of you have mirrors at home?” Tito Butch asked everyone.


Uhm, who doesn’t?


As I listened to Lesson 1’s intro, this question gripped me


“Is that the real you?”

You mean the ‘me’ I saw in the CCF mirror before the session started? I think so. Yes, that was the real me. Freshened up from the afternoon heat. Unexpectant but a little doubtful, excited but a bit nervous. Broke but happy (with one hand in my pocket like Alanis Morissette).

I filled in the blanks in my orange workbook as I followed the presentation slides. I’ve dived into words like “denial,” “powerlessness,” “compulsive behavior” and “unmanageable” many times before but I’m seeing them with a fresh pair of goggles this time. Seriously.

“I don’t have power. I don’t have influence.” I wrote these last week. I also remember writing that doubt is the one thing, the one challenge, which keeps me stuck.

Now, if powerlessness isn’t the issue but the denial of it and I actually recognize – and accept – that I don’t have power by writing it down, then what else could I be denying?

I don’t get it.

Ok. I know I’m powerless over WiFi, weather and traffic but that’s not the point, is it? What I’m powerless in is my knack for doubt and my hostile tendencies.


Tito Butch’s voice echoes: “You have the option to be a better person.”


I know I won’t “get it” right away but I have fully taken that option. In spite of my doubts, I am hopeful that a better version of me will come out of this. I want to get better. I want to be better. Thankfully, Jesus’ words in Matthew 9:12 assures me:

“It is not those who are healthy who need a physician, but those who are sick.”

I have to let that sink in. I’m ready with my pencil and my orange workbook is on page 5.


WRITE ABOUT IT the heading said.


So now I glance at the rear view mirror of my life, recalling situations or experiences which trigger an automatic reaction from me.


This feels weird. “I remember the boy, but I don’t remember the feeling anymore…”


Wait. Seriously?!

The Beginning

Words by Diwata, a GH Life Coach

The tables are all arranged. People wearing orange, green, and yellow – the Glorious Hope workers – are walking back and forth as we all prepared for the big event: the Glorious Hope Orientation.


The leaders have made their speeches and given their final announcements before we opened the registration table. As I prayed to God to set my heart right even before we start this year’s run, I am thankful for these words from our ministry head, Tito Butch, when he reminded everyone this truth:

“These people are not just numbers, not statistics… These are special people chosen by God.”

– Butch Mossesgeld

Indeed they are.


When I joined Glorious Hope as a participant, I was lost, confused, and didn’t know where else to go. There was nowhere else for me to go. Now, it’s my turn to share the comfort that was given me by God through this great program.


As I listened to the speakers during the orientation, I was reminded why I do what I do, why I serve and commit my attendance every Saturday, and at times, even on random days when a participant needs a listening ear. God has a purpose for my pain in the same way as God has a purpose for the pain of the participants that He has predestined to attend this year’s run.


The orientation is just the beginning. Even there I saw God working in our midst as He allowed Glorious Hope to overflow at the main lobby. With the live feed shown there, our borders were not limited to the guests alone. People passing by the lobby, together with CCF workers and guests, got to have the chance to learn about what Glorious Hope is all about.


I’m excited to see how the Lord will work in the lives of everyone involved. For me, it is always a privilege to be trusted with other people’s stories – with their lives and their hearts. More than that, it is a great privilege to be used by God to work for Him and with Him through this ministry.


As I end today’s entry, I want to share these things that struck me during the orientation as Tito Butch addressed this year’s participants:

“You will go back to the past not to dwell (on it), but (to) learn from your past (and) to make a difference in your future… Who you are now is not the same as who you are tomorrow.”

– Butch Mossesgeld

Get ready, everyone!


Gear up for a breakthrough!

The Challenge

Words by Coco, a GH Life Coach

I know what it’s like to be a winner. I also know what it’s like to lose.


I’ve tried several times to do something that I believed would give me success and change my life “forever”. I’ve unmasked myself and took many steps forward. I’ve felt what it was like to be so free, so alive. I’ve pressed my life’s undo button, too.

Yet something in me still doesn’t seem to click. There are days when I can’t bring myself up out of bed, too tired even when I’ve just woken up. My life, summed up, is not what most people would say blessed. I don’t have influence. I don’t have power.

I’ve been angry, so angry, for lots of reasons – some petty, some justifiable.

Strange things have happened to me, too, like when I threw my passport and boarding pass into the waste bin of a busy international airport toilet. I have no memory of actually having done it. Am I losing my marbles?

“What is the challenge that keeps you stuck?” Sel, the bubbly host, asked everyone to cap off Orientation Day. I knew many things are holding me back but I couldn’t find the words.

I stared at the green sheet of paper.


Under my name, I wrote the letters down.


D-O-U-B-T.


I sooo need a breakthrough.
